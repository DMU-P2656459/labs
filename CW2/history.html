Notable Guitarists

Here we go over some inflential guitar players, who they were, what they did and how they impacted music. Scroll down to the bottom for a quiz on the guitarists featured!

Les Paul

Les Paul, real name Lester William Polsfuss, was an American jazz and country guitarist and inventor who lived from June 9, 1915, in Waukesha, Wisconsin, to August 12, 2009, in White Plains, New York. He is best known for designing the solid-body electric guitar, but he also made significant contributions to the recording process. Les Paul, renowned as the "Godfather of the Modern Recording Studio," is credited with several discoveries and improvements that revolutionised the way musicians perform, engineers record, and the way fans hear music. Les Paul is arguably the most influential figure in the modern music industry's history. He was a pioneer in the invention of the solid body electric guitar, as well as a variety of recording techniques such as multi-track recording, digital delay, reverb, and more.

Chuck Berry

Chuck Berry, known as the "Father of Rock 'n' Roll," was exposed to music at a young age in school and church. He was sentenced to three years in jail as a youngster for armed robbery. He started creating songs in the 1950s, including "Johnny B. Goode" in 1958, and earned his first No. 1 hit with "My Ding-a-Ling" in 1972. Berry became one of the most influential people in rock music history thanks to his creative lyrics and distinctive sounds. Berry is still regarded as one of the most influential musicians in the genre. He was honoured with the Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 1985. He was the inaugural inductee into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame a year later, in 1986.

B.B. King

Riley B. King, better known by his stage name B.B. King, was an American blues singer, composer, and guitarist who lived from September 16, 1925 until May 14, 2015. 'Blues Boy' is abbreviated as B.B. Bukka White, a blues musician who commonly acknowledges B.B. King as an early instructor and mentor, is a cousin of B.B. King. The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inducted King in 1987. King was also inducted into the R&B Music Hall of Fame's 2014 class. He is known as "The King of Blues" and one of the "Three Kings of the Blues Guitar," and is considered one of the most influential blues guitarists of all time (along with Albert King and Freddie King). King was also noted for giving nonstop performances throughout his musical career, averaging over 200 concerts a year until his 70s. He allegedly performed in 342 gigs in 1956. President George H.W. Bush gave King the National Medal of Arts in 1990. President George W. Bush awarded him the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2006.

Jimmy Page

Jimmy Page is an English musician best known for being the guitarist and founding member of the iconic rock band 'Led Zeppelin.' He began his career as a studio session musician in London, where he is also a superb songwriter and multi-instrumentalist. Elvis Presley influenced him as a young lad, and he began playing the guitar at the age of 12. Though he had some instruction at first, he was mostly self-taught. He started to play in local halls as a teenager, and at the age of 15, he joined the band 'The Crusaders.' However, due to an illness, he had to put his musical career on hold and decided to concentrate on his education. But he couldn't stay away from music for long, and he began performing while still a student. Within a few years, he had established himself as a talented instrumentalist, and with singer Robert Plant, bassist and keyboardist John Paul Jones, and drummer John Bonham, he formed the rock band 'Led Zeppelin.' The band went on to become known as the forefathers of heavy metal and hard rock, and they had tremendous success.
